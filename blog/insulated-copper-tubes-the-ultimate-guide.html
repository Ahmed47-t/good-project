Insulated Copper Tubes: The Ultimate Guide for HVAC Efficiency & Durability

BENRO INDUSTRIES·February 25, 2025

What Are Insulated Copper Tubes?

Insulated copper tubes are the backbone of modern HVAC and refrigeration systems. They consist of a seamless copper pipe wrapped in high-performance insulation (like polyethylene foam) to minimise heat transfer, prevent condensation and enhance energy efficiency.

Why Copper?

  • Superior Thermal Conductivity — copper transfers heat 20× faster than aluminium.
  • Corrosion Resistance — ideal for harsh climates like North Africa’s desert regions.
  • Durability — withstands extreme temperatures and pressures.

Key Components

  1. Copper Core — high-purity C12200 copper (99.9% Cu) compliant with EN 12735-1.
  2. Insulation Layer — PE foam (thermal conductivity: 0.0402 W/m·K) or rubber.
  3. Protective Jacket — UV-resistant, chemical-proof outer layer.

Applications of Insulated Copper Tubes

1. Residential Air Conditioning

  • Split AC Systems — pre-insulated tubes connect indoor/outdoor units.
  • Central HVAC — minimise energy loss in ductwork.

2. Commercial & Industrial Projects

  • Hospitals — maintain sterile environments with leak-proof connections.
  • Cold Storage — insulated tubes for refrigeration (e.g. غرف تبريد).

3. Renewable Energy

  • Heat Pumps — efficient heat transfer in geothermal systems.

5 Benefits of Insulated Copper Tubes

  1. Energy Savings — reduce cooling costs by up to 30% (ASHRAE Study).
  2. Condensation Control — PE foam prevents moisture build-up.
  3. Longevity — resists UV, chemicals and abrasion.
  4. Easy Installation — pre-insulated coils cut labour time by 50%.
  5. Eco-Friendly — recyclable materials align with Algeria’s sustainability goals.

Step 2: Select Insulation Thickness

  • PE Foam — 9 mm (standard) to 25 mm (extreme climates).
  • Rubber — better flexibility for tight spaces.

Step 3: Verify Certifications

  • Look for EN 12735-1 or ASTM B280 compliance.
  • Ensure fire ratings (e.g. ASTM E84 Class 1).

Installation Best Practices

  1. Avoid Kinks — use tube benders for smooth curves.
  2. Seal Joints — apply UV-resistant tape at connections.
  3. Test for Leaks — pressure-test with nitrogen before refrigerant charging.
